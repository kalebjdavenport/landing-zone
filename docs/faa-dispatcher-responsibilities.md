# FAA Certified Aircraft Dispatcher — Responsibilities

## Overview

An FAA certified aircraft dispatcher (ADX certificate holder) shares **joint legal responsibility** with the pilot-in-command (PIC) for the safe conduct of every flight under 14 CFR Part 121. The dispatcher is the only person other than the PIC who holds this authority — both must agree before a flight can be released.

---

## Regulatory Foundation

- **14 CFR Part 65, Subpart C** — Dispatcher certification (eligibility, knowledge test, practical test)
- **14 CFR Part 121, Subpart T (§121.531–121.537)** — Operational control and joint responsibility
- **14 CFR Part 121, Subpart U (§121.599–121.667)** — Dispatching and flight release rules
- **FAA AC 120-101** — Detailed guidance on operational control

Under **§121.533**, the PIC and dispatcher are jointly responsible for preflight planning, delay, and dispatch release. Either party can refuse to dispatch, and that refusal is legally protected.

---

## Pre-Flight Duties

- **Route selection** — Choose the safest and most efficient route considering winds, weather, airspace, and traffic
- **Weather analysis** — Review METARs, TAFs, SIGMETs, AIRMETs, PIREPs, radar, satellite imagery, winds aloft, and prognostic charts
- **NOTAM review** — Check for runway closures, navaid outages, airspace restrictions, and other impacts at all airports along the route
- **Fuel planning** — Calculate taxi, trip, contingency, alternate, holding, and reserve fuel based on aircraft type, payload, winds, and weather
- **Weight and balance** — Verify the aircraft is within takeoff/landing weight limits and center-of-gravity envelope
- **Alternate airports** — Identify and plan alternates in case the destination becomes unavailable
- **Dispatch release** — Prepare and sign the legal authorization for the flight; the PIC must also concur

---

## In-Flight Duties

- **Flight monitoring** — Continuously track the progress of each dispatched flight (a typical dispatcher monitors 10–25 flights simultaneously)
- **Weather updates** — Monitor developing weather along routes and at destination/alternate airports; relay changes to the crew via ACARS, company radio, or SATCOM
- **Re-dispatch and diversion** — Under §121.533(c), the dispatcher can cancel or redispatch a flight if it cannot continue safely as planned
- **ETOPS monitoring** — For extended operations, continuously evaluate weather at ETOPS alternates and ensure the aircraft remains within authorized diversion time
- **Crew communication** — Advise crews of turbulence reports, reroutes, airport delays, or mechanical concerns

---

## Post-Flight Duties

- Review flight logs for delays, irregularities, or safety concerns
- Maintain records of dispatch releases for regulatory compliance
- Remain on duty until each dispatched flight completes, exits the dispatcher's jurisdiction, or is handed off to another qualified dispatcher

## Data Sources and Tools

| Source | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **METARs / SPECIs** | Current surface observations at airports |
| **TAFs** | Terminal forecasts (6–30 hr outlook) |
| **SIGMETs / AIRMETs** | Significant weather warnings (icing, turbulence, convection, volcanic ash) |
| **PIREPs** | Pilot reports of actual in-flight conditions |
| **NOTAMs** | Airport, airspace, and navaid status notices |
| **Winds/temps aloft** | Upper-level wind and temperature forecasts for fuel and route planning |
| **Radar and satellite** | Real-time precipitation and cloud imagery |
| **ACARS** | Digital data link for crew–dispatcher messaging |
| **ADS-B** | Real-time aircraft position tracking |

Primary weather source: **Aviation Weather Center** (aviationweather.gov), backed by **NWS**.

---

## Certification Requirements

- **Age**: 23 years old to hold the certificate
- **Training**: Minimum 200 hours at an FAA-approved dispatcher school (Part 65, Appendix A)
- **Knowledge test**: Comprehensive written exam comparable in difficulty to the ATP written
- **Practical test**: Oral and practical exam with an FAA Designated Examiner
- **Recurrent**: At least 5 hours of flight deck observation per 12 months, plus approved recurrent training including Dispatcher Resource Management (DRM)

---

## Coordination and Operational Control

- **With pilots** — Joint pre-flight briefing and concurrence on the flight plan; in-flight advisory role
- **With ATC** — Coordinate ground delays, ground stops, and flow control through Traffic Management Units (not direct ATC communication during flight)
- **With operations** — Central hub in the Operations Control Center connecting maintenance, crew scheduling, station ops, fueling, and customer service

The dispatcher exercises day-to-day operational control: the authority to initiate, conduct, divert, delay, or terminate a flight in the interest of safety.

---

## Key Principle

The co-authority dispatch model functions as a checks-and-balances system — two qualified professionals independently evaluating whether a flight can be conducted safely, one from the ground and one from the cockpit.
